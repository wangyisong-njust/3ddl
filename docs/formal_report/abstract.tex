Accelerating deep-learning-based 3D semiconductor inspection is commonly evaluated by patch-level latency or segmentation overlap alone. However, when the downstream application applies thresholded metrology---computing bond-line thickness, pad misalignment, and solder extrusion from predicted masks---even small boundary shifts can flip binary defect flags. This creates a \emph{deployment gap}: a model that appears fast and accurate at the segmentation level can still produce unacceptable behavior after integration into the full inspection workflow.

To address this gap, this report proposes a \textbf{Two-Level Deployment-Safety Gate}. Gate~1 screens candidate accelerations on a 174-case segmentation evaluation set, requiring preservation of class-sensitive Dice. Gate~2 re-evaluates survivors inside the complete \emph{intelliscan} pipeline on raw 3D X-ray scans, requiring zero defect-flag flips, bounded metrology drift, and stable class~3 and class~4 Dice. Only methods that pass both gates are accepted for deployment.

Applying this methodology yields two main findings. First, a sequence of parity-preserving engineering optimizations---combined in-memory segmentation and metrology, exact quantized in-memory detection, CPU parallelism, and batch prefetch---achieves a 45.13\% mean total runtime reduction across eight validated raw scans with \emph{exact} output parity. Second, structured pruning with class-aware knowledge distillation produces a Pareto front of compressed models; yet even the best student (full-case average Dice 0.8895) fails Gate~2 with large defect-flag flips upon whole-pipeline deployment, revealing a deployment gap that Dice alone cannot detect.

The principal contribution is therefore not a single faster model, but a reusable evaluation methodology: for threshold-sensitive industrial pipelines, any acceleration must be validated through both model-level quality and whole-pipeline defect stability before it can be considered deployment-safe.
